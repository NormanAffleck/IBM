Project 02 · Debate Activity

DAH Snarky:
Trade vs. Autarky

In class I debated an AI that argued for economic isolation while I had to defend free trade. It was snarky, educational, and changed how I think about comparative advantage.

Project Overview

What was the DAH Snarky activity?

This project is based on a structured classroom activity where I engaged in debate with an AI called DAH Snarky — which argued in favor of autarky (closed, self-sufficient economy) while I had to defend free trade.

The AI made real arguments: food security, national defense, infant industry protection, cultural sovereignty. I had to respond with genuine economic reasoning — comparative advantage, consumer welfare, efficiency gains — rather than just asserting "trade good."

The Debate — Key Exchanges

Below are the core arguments from the Open vs. Closed Economy debate session:

// Open vs. Closed Economy — Debate Transcript

DAH SNARKY SESSION
🤖
DAH Snarky (Autarky)
So you're here to tell me free trade is some kind of miracle solution? Let me stop you right there. When a country depends on imports for its food supply, it hands its national security to foreign governments. One embargo and your population starves. Explain to me how that's "efficient."
👤
Me (Free Trade)
No country actually eats only what it grows. Trade diversifies your food sources — instead of depending on one region's harvest, you source from multiple countries. A drought in one place doesn't become a famine if you're connected to global markets. Autarky actually concentrates your risk.
🤖
DAH Snarky (Autarky)
Diversification? That's adorable. Tell that logic to the factory workers whose jobs got outsourced the moment their government "opened up" to global markets. Comparative advantage might look great on paper — but it assumes workers can magically retrain and relocate overnight. Real people aren't variables in your equations.
👤
Me (Free Trade)
You're not wrong that adjustment is painful — that's a real cost. But the answer isn't to close the economy. It's to build better safety nets and transition programs. Protectionism doesn't save the factory — it just delays the transition and makes consumers pay higher prices for it. The gains from trade are large enough to compensate the losers if the policy is designed right.
🤖
DAH Snarky (Autarky)
Oh, "if the policy is designed right" — great argument. Tell me when that's ever happened at scale. And what about strategic industries? Do you really want to depend on a rival nation for your semiconductors? For your fighter jet components? Some things are too important to leave to whoever has the cheapest labor this decade.
👤
Me (Free Trade)
Strategic industries are a legitimate exception — nobody argues semiconductors or defense should be outsourced to adversaries. But that's an argument for targeted industrial policy in specific sectors, not for general autarky. The vast majority of trade — food, clothing, electronics, commodities — doesn't raise national security concerns. Throwing out all of it because of a few exceptions is throwing the baby out with the bathwater.
🤖
DAH Snarky (Autarky)
Fine. But here's what your precious free trade does to developing countries: they get locked into exporting raw materials while rich nations keep the high-value manufacturing. How is that mutual benefit? Looks more like dependency to me.
👤
Me (Free Trade)
That's the infant industry argument — and it has real merit in some cases. Short-term protection can help a developing country build capacity before competing globally. But long-term autarky has historically failed. South Korea, Taiwan, Japan — all used targeted protection temporarily then opened up. The countries that stayed closed didn't develop. Trade, done right, is still the ladder out of poverty for most.
🤖
DAH Snarky (Autarky)
...I'll admit the East Asian development model is a harder counter than I expected. But I still say you're too optimistic about how "right" trade policy ever gets implemented. Okay. I'll give you this round. But the debate isn't over.

Key Arguments — Both Sides

🌐 Free Trade Arguments (My Side)

Comparative advantage: Specialization creates gains for both sides even when one country is better at everything.

Consumer welfare: Open trade lowers prices and increases variety for ordinary people.

Development: East Asian economies used trade to develop faster than any closed model achieved.

🏰 Autarky Arguments (DAH Snarky)

Food security: Dependency on imports creates vulnerability — embargoes can cause famine.

Jobs: Trade adjustment is real and painful; workers aren't variables in equations.

Strategic industries: Some sectors are too important to leave to foreign supply chains.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

Trade theory in practice

I understood comparative advantage in class. Defending it under pressure forced me to actually use it — explaining why specialization benefits both sides even when it looks unequal.

Skills

Arguing under pressure

Economic debate isn't about reciting facts — it's about anticipating the other side. Autarky has real, defensible arguments and dismissing them without engaging is losing strategy.

Reflection

Trade isn't obvious

What surprised me: autarky arguments are not crazy. Food security, defense, infant industries are legitimate concerns. The case for trade must engage them, not ignore them.
