Chapter 10 | Leadership and Politics in Organizations

üîπ Trait Theories of Leadership

  • Leadership is the ability to influence a group toward a goal.
  • Leadership can come from formal roles or personal influence.
  • Traits like extraversion, conscientiousness, and emotional intelligence are linked to leadership.
  • Two key conclusions:
    • Traits can predict leadership.
    • Traits help identify leaders more than differentiate effectiveness.

üîπ Behavioral Theories of Leadership

  • Leadership behaviors can be learned and trained.
  • Two key behaviors:
    • Initiating structure: Defining roles and tasks.
    • Consideration: Building trust, respect, and regard for others.

üîπ Contingency Theories

  • Fiedler Model: Match leader's style (task/relationship) to the situation.
  • SLT (Situational Leadership Theory): Leaders adapt based on followers' readiness.
  • Path-Goal Theory: Leader's job is to assist followers in reaching goals.
  • Leader-Participation Model: Decision-making participation depends on task structure.

üîπ Charismatic and Transformational Leadership

  • Charismatic Leaders: Have a strong vision, personal risk, sensitivity to followers, and inspire through behavior.
  • They influence by articulating a compelling vision and showing conviction.
  • Transformational Leaders: Inspire, stimulate, and give attention to individuals.
  • Organizations often perform better with transformational leadership.

üîπ Ethical and Positive Leadership

  • Abusive Supervision: Hostile behaviors by leaders can harm employees.
  • Trust: A psychological state where one accepts vulnerability based on positive expectations.
  • Trust Development:
    • Trust propensity (natural tendency)
    • Role of culture and past behavior
    • Can be rebuilt through consistent trustworthy actions
