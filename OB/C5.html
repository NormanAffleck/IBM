Chapter 5 | Personality and Individual Differences

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand person–job and person–organization fit.
  • Describe personality traits and how they're measured.
  • Evaluate MBTI, Big Five, and Dark Triad frameworks.
  • Explain core self-evaluation, self-monitoring, and proactive personality.
  • Understand situation strength and trait activation theory.
  • Explore intellectual and physical abilities in OB.
  • Differentiate terminal and instrumental values.

🔄 Fit Theories

  • Person–Job Fit: Matching personality types to job types (Holland’s theory).
  • Person–Organization Fit: People gravitate toward organizations with aligned values.
  • Also includes Person–Group and Person–Supervisor fit.

🧠 Defining and Measuring Personality

  • Personality: Sum of ways an individual interacts with the environment.
  • Traits: Enduring behavior patterns.
  • Measured via self-report surveys and assessments.

📊 Frameworks: MBTI, Big Five, Dark Triad

  • MBTI: Popular but lacks strong validity. Classifies into 16 types (e.g., INTJ, ESTJ).
  • Big Five: Conscientiousness, Emotional Stability, Extraversion, Openness, Agreeableness. Strong empirical support.
  • Dark Triad: Machiavellianism, Narcissism, Psychopathy — linked to toxic workplace behavior.
  • Other Models: DiSC and HEXACO (adds Honesty-Humility).

👤 Key Traits

  • Core Self-Evaluation (CSE): Belief in own competence and worth.
  • Self-Monitoring: Ability to adjust behavior to the situation.
  • Proactive Personality: Tendency to take initiative and drive change.

📈 Situation and Behavior

  • Situation Strength Theory: Personality shows more in weak situations than strong ones.
  • Trait Activation Theory: Some contexts activate specific traits more than others.

🧠 Abilities in OB

  • Intellectual Abilities: General mental ability (GMA), measured by tests like Wonderlic.
  • Physical Abilities: Stamina, strength, flexibility, and other job-related capacities.

💎 Values

  • Terminal Values: Desired end goals (e.g., peace, happiness).
  • Instrumental Values: Means to achieve those goals (e.g., honesty, ambition).
  • Generational Values: Perceived (not proven) differences among age groups.

📋 Implications for Managers

  • Evaluate job and organizational fit with personality traits.
  • Use validated frameworks (e.g., Big Five) in hiring decisions.
  • Be aware of trait relevance in training and performance.
  • Account for situational factors in observed behavior.
  • Be cautious with ability tests to avoid legal risks.
  • Avoid stereotyping employees by generation.
